Almost one year ago I started to consistently work on
[Servo](https://servo.org/) issues in my spare time and besides learning Rust
and having tons of fun, it taught me to become a better developer in so many
ways. Mostly because the Servo team members are truly amazing people, who
openly share their passion for technicalities with newcomers and make the
overall contributing experience very pleasant, intriguing and potentially
addictive. But as much as I love to talk about how amazing Mozillians are (dare
ask my wife about it), in this post I'm focusing on the most valuable thing
I've learned by contributing.

# Providing meaningful context
My initial motivation of contributing to Servo was to learn [Rust]().
To my surprise that is the thing I've learned the least in
the last year of contributing (I'm quite confident reading Rust code, but
writing Rust code is a whole different story). What I actually learned to be the
most valuable lesson is the importantance of communicating context to fellow
developers.

As a newcomer I have many questions when working on an issue and I don't want
the reponse to my supposedly well crafted question be: "Can you elaborate?"
or "Can you provide more context?" That's no fun. Especially not if you have
to wait at least 8 hours for a response, because the other developer lives in a
different time zone. So putting the problem with all its context in my head
into such precise words, that the reader will instantly understand my problem
without having to ask any more questions, should be the goal here.
The more context I'll provide and the more precise that
context describes the problem I'm having, the more approachable it is for
someone else to respond and the greater the chances of the response solving
my problem.
